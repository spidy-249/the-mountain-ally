Why We Climb

By Jyotiranjan Barman

People often ask me why I leave the comfort of the city, the safety of a warm bed, and the predictability of daily life for the harsh winds of the Himalayas. They ask if I do it for the view.

But if it were just about the view, I could look at a photograph. No, I don't go to the mountains to look at them. I go to the mountains to live.

It's Not a Vacation. It's Life.

To me, traveling in the mountains isn't an escape from reality; it is the only time reality feels sharp enough to cut. In the city, we exist in a blur of notifications, traffic, and routine. We are sleepwalking.

But when you are on a ridge line at 14,000 feet, gasping for thin air, putting one heavy boot in front of the other, you are undeniably awake. You feel every heartbeat. You feel the cold on your skin. That struggle isn't suffering—it's the proof that you are alive.

"The mountains don't care about your resume, your money, or your problems. They only care about your honesty."

The True Traveler Loves the Road

There is a difference between a tourist and a traveler. A tourist is obsessed with the destination—the summit selfie, the final point.

But a true traveler falls in love with the journey itself. It’s about the tea you share with a shepherd in a rain-soaked tent. It’s about the silence of the pine forest before dawn. It’s about the moment your legs want to give up, but your spirit refuses.

I started The Mountain Ally because I wanted to share this feeling. I don't want to just guide you to a peak. I want to be there with you when you realize that the person who started the climb is not the same person standing at the top.

We climb because down here, we exist. But up there? Up there, we truly live.
